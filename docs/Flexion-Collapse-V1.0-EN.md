# 2. Structural Limits of Existence

A structure exists only while its state  
\[
X = (\Delta, \Phi, M, \kappa)
\]

The viability domain is the region of the Δ–Φ–M–κ space where structural coherence is possible.  
Within this domain:

- Δ retains a stable geometric configuration,  
- Φ can be redistributed without rupture,  
- M grows at a non-zero rate,  
- and κ remains strictly positive.

Outside this domain, the system cannot maintain structural definability.

Each structural variable has its own collapse threshold:

- **Δ-threshold:** excessive concentration or instability of substance leads to geometric breakdown.  
- **Φ-threshold:** tension or energy gradients exceed the structure’s ability to redistribute stress.

## 2.3 Structural stability as κ > 0
The viability parameter κ encodes the system’s remaining capacity to maintain coherence.  
As long as κ > 0:

- Δ remains structurally meaningful,  
- Φ-distribution is possible,  
- temporal evolution continues,  
- and collapse cannot occur.

κ functions as the universal indicator of structural health.

## 2.4 Approaching the collapse regime
As Δ, Φ, and M approach their thresholds, κ decreases.  
This produces observable structural symptoms:

- increasing Δ-instability (mass concentration, material weakness),  
- rising Φ-tension (energetic overload),  
- slowing temporal evolution (dM/dt → 0),  
- reduced field coherence.

These tendencies foreshadow entry into the collapse boundary.

## 2.5 Breakdown tendencies
Near the structural limit, the system becomes hypersensitive to perturbations.  
Small deviations in Δ, Φ, or M produce large changes in κ.  
This hypersensitivity marks the onset of breakdown: the point at which the structure is no longer able to preserve coherent identity and begins its transition to collapse.

The structural limits of existence define the boundary between coherent reality and the onset of structural death.

- plasma-driven breakdowns,

- aging of physical systems,

- Φ-stress accelerates M-growth,  
- M-growth reduces κ,  
- κ-reduction amplifies Δ and Φ instability.
