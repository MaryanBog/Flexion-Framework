# 3. Flexion Dynamics (FD)

Flexion Dynamics is the foundational motion layer of the Flexion Framework. It defines how structures evolve within the structural state space, how deviation changes over time, how energy and memory emerge from motion, and how contractivity governs the direction and reversibility of structural evolution. FD provides the dynamic engine that drives the behavior of any structured system.

### 3.1 Role in the Framework
Flexion Dynamics describes the rules of structural motion. It defines how a system transitions from one state to another under the influence of deviation, energy, memory, and contractivity. FD establishes the dynamic backbone upon which structural geometry (FST), fields (FFT), and time (FTT) are built.

### 3.2 Deviation Evolution
Deviation ∆ is the primary driver of structural motion. The deviation evolution rule determines how ∆ changes as the structure moves through its state space. In the contractive regime, deviation tends toward symmetry; in the expansive regime, deviation grows and accelerates structural instability. Deviation governs the trajectory of the system and determines its approach toward equilibrium or collapse.

### 3.3 Energy and Motion
Structural energy Φ measures the tension that arises from deviation. Energy interacts with deviation to shape dynamic behavior, modifying the speed and curvature of motion. Increased energy compresses the dynamic landscape and accelerates structural changes. Energy accumulation may push the system toward instability if it approaches its upper limit Φ_max.

### 3.4 Memory and Irreversibility
Memory M accumulates as a result of dynamic activity. Memory introduces irreversible drift into the system, making backward motion longer or impossible. High memory reduces stability, shrinks the Viability Domain, and increases temporal curvature. Irreversibility arises naturally as memory grows, marking the structural arrow of time inside the dynamic regime.

### 3.5 Directional Parameter σ
The directional parameter σ determines whether the system operates in a contractive or expansive regime. In the contractive regime (σ = −1), deviation tends to decrease and the structure moves toward symmetry. In the expansive regime (σ = +1), deviation grows, accelerating divergence and reducing stability. σ defines the global direction of structural evolution.

### 3.6 Collapse Dynamics
Collapse occurs when the system approaches the boundary where κ → 0. As contractivity diminishes, dynamic speed increases and motion becomes unstable. Collapse dynamics are characterized by diverging deviation, rising energy, accelerating memory accumulation, and geometric instability. The system loses the ability to reverse motion and transitions into structural death.

### 3.7 Interactions with FST, FFT, FTT
Flexion Dynamics interacts with the remaining layers of the Framework:
- With FST, FD determines geodesic motion and the deformation of structural space.
- With FFT, FD evolves under fields generated by deviation, energy, memory, and contractivity.
- With FTT, FD generates the temporal flow through structural change.

Together, these interactions make Flexion Dynamics the engine of the unified structural system.

Curvature in Flexion Space arises from the nonlinear influence of the four structural variables:
- deviation ∆ produces radial curvature and geometric stretching,
- energy Φ introduces compressive curvature and tension wells,
- memory M generates asymmetric curvature and irreversibility,
- contractivity κ stabilizes the geometry and prevents metric degeneration.
As any variable approaches its critical limit, curvature can diverge and destabilize structural motion.

The Viability Domain is the subset of Flexion Space in which structural existence is possible. It is defined by upper bounds on deviation, energy, and memory, and by non-negative contractivity:
  
Φ ≤ Φ_max,  
M ≤ M_max,  
‖∆‖ ≤ ∆_max,

The collapse boundary ∂D is the hypersurface where κ = 0. At this boundary, the metric degenerates, curvature diverges, geodesics terminate, and the structure begins transitioning into collapse. ∂D marks the limit of structural viability and the onset of geometric breakdown.

### 4.6 Geodesics
Geodesics in Flexion Space describe the natural paths of structural motion under the state-dependent metric. Their behavior is influenced by all structural variables and can diverge, bend, compress, or terminate depending on local curvature. Near the collapse boundary, geodesics shorten and ultimately disappear as the manifold loses smoothness and continuity.

### 4.7 Spatial Interpretation of Collapse
Collapse corresponds to the geometric failure of Flexion Space. As κ → 0, the metric flattens or degenerates, curvature becomes singular, and the manifold loses its differentiable structure. Motion becomes undefined, the temporal field disappears, and the structure enters the collapse manifold where no meaningful geometry persists.

Flexion Space Theory thus defines the geometric backbone of the entire Framework, determining where structural motion is possible and how it evolves toward stability or collapse.
